On the Recursive Modelling of the Human Telencephalon

Author: Elias Verne
Affiliation: Independent Researcher

Status: Withdrawn Preprint
Submitted: 2019-05-14
Published: —

Abstract

The human telencephalon represents the most structurally and functionally complex region of the mammalian brain, serving as the primary substrate for higher-order cognitive processes. This manuscript proposes a recursive abstraction model intended to describe hierarchical information processing within telencephalic structures. The model attempts to bridge anatomical organization and symbolic representation, suggesting that cognition emerges from iterative self-referential loops rather than linear signal propagation.

1. Introduction

Contemporary models of cognition frequently rely on modular or network-based approaches to explain the functional organization of the brain. While such models have proven useful, they often fail to account for the apparent continuity between perception, abstraction, and symbolic reasoning observed in human cognition. The telencephalon, encompassing the cerebral cortex and associated subcortical structures, provides a suitable domain for investigating these limitations.

Previous work has emphasized localized specialization within the telencephalon; however, emerging evidence suggests that cognitive phenomena may arise from recursive interactions across multiple hierarchical levels. This work seeks to formalize such interactions into a conceptual and mathematical framework.

2. Theoretical Framework

The proposed model treats telencephalic processing as a series of recursive transformations applied to representational states. Each transformation layer operates on the output of the previous layer, while simultaneously influencing its own input through feedback mechanisms. This recursive structure is hypothesized to enable abstraction, self-reference, and symbolic manipulation.

Rather than assigning discrete cognitive functions to isolated regions, the model emphasizes patterns of recurrence and convergence. In this view, meaning is not encoded at a single locus, but emerges through repeated iteration across scales.

3. Implications for Cognitive Modelling

If cognition is indeed grounded in recursive telencephalic processes, then algorithmic descriptions of intelligence may be better understood as rediscoveries of biological principles rather than artificial constructs. Such a perspective challenges prevailing distinctions between natural and artificial cognition, suggesting a deeper structural correspondence.

These implications extend beyond neuroscience into fields such as artificial intelligence, philosophy of mind, and formal logic. However, rigorous empirical validation remains necessary.

4. Limitations and Withdrawal

The present manuscript was withdrawn prior to formal peer review following methodological disputes concerning the empirical tractability of the proposed framework. Specifically, concerns were raised regarding the operational definition of recursion within biological substrates and the lack of direct experimental validation.

Despite these limitations, the author maintains that the conceptual model warrants further consideration as a heuristic for understanding higher-order cognition.

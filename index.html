On the Recursive Modelling of the Human Telencephalon

Elias Verne
Independent Researcher
Submitted: 14 May 2019
Status: Withdrawn

Abstract

This paper investigates the role of the human telencephalon in the recognition and stabilization of numerical and structural patterns. Drawing on insights from computational neuroscience and formal systems theory, the study proposes that the ability to identify quantities, sequences, and ordered relationships precedes linguistic or semantic interpretation.

Cognitive processes are modeled as enumerable units governed by recursive constraints, where repetition and invariant structure provide coherence independently of meaning. Within this framework, numerical organization is treated as a primary cognitive substrate rather than a symbolic layer derived from language.

Introduction

Prevailing accounts of higher cognition frequently emphasize meaning, intention, and representational content. However, accumulating evidence suggests that the telencephalon supports higher reasoning through the detection of regularities, proportions, and repeatable patterns that can be formally described.

Such patterns may be enumerated, ordered, and recombined without reference to semantic interpretation, indicating that structure itself may act as a sufficient organizing principle for complex cognitive activity.

Methodological Framework

The methodological approach adopted in this work deliberately minimizes interpretive semantics. Instead, it focuses on formal constraints, recursive operations, and invariant relationships between discrete units. These units are treated as countable elements whose organization can be expressed numerically and examined independently of linguistic meaning.

Discussion

Preliminary analysis indicates that recursive stability is preserved even when semantic content is removed entirely. The persistence of numerical regularity under such conditions supports the hypothesis that structural patterning constitutes the primary substrate of higher cognitive integration within the telencephalon.

Withdrawal Notice
This manuscript was withdrawn prior to formal peer review. The author concluded that several components of the analysis required further verification before public dissemination.
